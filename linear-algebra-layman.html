Chapter 01

The Problem That Started Everything

Mathematics has always been a tool before it was an art. Linear algebra was born from a simple, urgent, practical need: how do you solve many equations at once?

Begin with ordinary algebra. You have one equation, one unknown: 2x = 6. Divide both sides. Done. One answer, always.

Now scale it up. You have a hundred products, a hundred prices, a hundred supply constraints — all tangled together. Writing them out one by one is hopeless. You need a machine. You need a language that can hold all of this simultaneously and manipulate it cleanly.

That machine is linear algebra. It was not invented in a philosopher's study — it was forced into existence by engineers, astronomers, and economists who needed to handle many variables changing together at the same time.

Every great mathematical tool was once just a desperate solution to a problem nobody else could solve.

The central question that drives the entire subject is this: can I find values that satisfy a set of equations? And if yes, how many solutions exist? Everything you will read in this essay — vectors, matrices, eigenvalues, neural networks — is an answer to some part of that question.

Chapter 02

Vectors & Matrices — The Machine

Before you can solve a system of equations, you need a language that can express one. That language has two words: vector and matrix.

A vector is simply a list of numbers — a direction and a magnitude in some space. Think of it as an arrow. The unknowns you're solving for form a vector. The targets you're trying to reach form another vector.

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers. But its true identity is not a grid — it is a compact way to represent an entire system of equations. Instead of writing out ten equations with ten unknowns, you write one matrix equation: Ax = b. The matrix A holds all the coefficients. The vector x holds all the unknowns. The vector b holds all the targets.

// A system of two equations:
2x₁ + 1x₂ = 5
3x₁ + 4x₂ = 6

// Written as a matrix equation Ax = b:
[2 1] [x₁] [5]
[3 4] [x₂] = [6]

The matrix is not just a notational shortcut. It is a transformation machine — a function that takes a vector in and produces a vector out. This realisation, when it fully lands, changes how you see every equation you will ever write.

Chapter 03

The Crisis: Three Possible Worlds

You set up your matrix equation. You try to solve it. And you discover something uncomfortable: there are three completely different things that can happen.

When you try to solve Ax = b, the universe splits into three possible outcomes. Not two — three. And figuring out which world you're in, and why, requires every concept that comes after.

World I

Exactly One Solution

The equations all point at a single, unique answer. The system is determined. This is the clean, happy case we hope for.

World II

No Solution

The equations contradict each other. They describe a target that is impossible to reach. No matter what x you try, Ax ≠ b.

World III — The Interesting One

Infinitely Many Solutions

Some equations are secretly copies of others, just rescaled or combined. They don't add new information. The system is underdetermined — there are free variables, whole dimensions of freedom. This case is where most of linear algebra's depth lives.

Why do these three cases exist? What determines which world you land in? That is the question that forces all the remaining concepts into existence. We needed a framework to think about the structure of equations — not just their solutions. That framework is the vector space.

Chapter 04

Vector Spaces — Building the Universe

To understand why equations behave the way they do, we need to understand the space those equations live in.

A vector space is a set of vectors with two clean operations: addition and scalar multiplication. You can add any two vectors and stay inside the set. You can scale any vector by any number and stay inside the set. There is always a zero vector.

Think of it as the universe where your problem lives. ℝ² is the flat plane. ℝ³ is ordinary 3D space. ℝⁿ is the generalisation to any number of dimensions — strange to visualise, but mathematically identical in behaviour.

The vector space doesn't tell you the answer to your equations. But it tells you the rules of the game — what operations are valid, what the possible answers look like, what constraints the solutions must satisfy.

A vector space is not a place you go to. It is the structure that makes mathematical reasoning possible in the first place.

Chapter 05

Independence, Span & Basis

Three concepts that answer the same question from three different angles: how many genuinely different directions does a set of vectors contain?

Concept I

Linear Independence

A set of vectors is linearly independent if no vector in the set can be made from the others. Each one adds a genuinely new direction. Dependent vectors are redundant — they're already secretly present in the others. Redundant equations produce underdetermined systems: infinitely many solutions.

Concept II

Span

The span of a set of vectors is everything you can reach by scaling and adding them in every possible combination. It is the smallest subspace containing those vectors. Ask: "given these directions, what is every location I can build?" The answer is the span.

Concept III

Basis

A basis is the minimal set of independent vectors whose span covers the entire space. No waste, no gaps. It is the most efficient description possible. The number of vectors in any basis for a space is always the same — and that number is the dimension of the space.

The Insight

Span is always a subspace

The span of any set of vectors satisfies all three subspace requirements automatically. It contains the zero vector (set all scalars to zero). It's closed under addition. It's closed under scaling. This is not a coincidence — it falls directly from the definitions. The span is the smallest subspace that contains those vectors.

Chapter 06

Rank — Counting What Matters

Rank is the single number that captures the essence of a matrix — how much real information it contains, stripped of all redundancy.

The rank of a matrix is the number of genuinely independent columns it has. It is simultaneously three things: the number of pivot columns after row reduction, the dimension of the column space (the image), and the number of linearly independent rows.

Rank answers the fundamental question of the three-case crisis directly. If rank equals the number of unknowns and the system is consistent — exactly one solution. If the system is inconsistent regardless — no solution. If rank is less than the number of unknowns — infinitely many solutions, with exactly (n − rank) free variables.

// The Rank-Nullity Theorem — a conservation law for dimensions:

dim(Kernel) + dim(Image) = n
    nullity + rank = dimension of domain

// Whatever A destroys (kernel) plus whatever it produces (image)
// must add up to the total dimensions you started with.

The kernel is everything the transformation destroys — all vectors that map to zero. The word comes from the German "Kern" meaning core or seed. The image is everything the transformation can produce — all possible outputs. Every concept you've seen so far is connected through this single conservation law.

The slope m you learned in school
was always a 1×1 matrix.
You just didn't know it yet.

In y = mx, the coefficient m stretches the number line. In Ax = b, the matrix A stretches, rotates, and projects space itself. Same mathematical structure, higher dimension. Linear algebra is just algebra that grew up.

Chapter 07

The Revelation: Matrices Are Transformations

At some point in learning linear algebra, the perspective shifts completely. The matrix stops being a grid of numbers and starts being a function that reshapes space itself.

A linear transformation T: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ is a function from one vector space to another. It takes a vector as input and produces a vector as output. Every matrix defines exactly one linear transformation — and every linear transformation can be represented by exactly one matrix (given a choice of bases).

The matrix A is not just a system of coefficients. It is a machine that transforms space. Depending on its entries, it can rotate a plane, stretch space along an axis, project three dimensions onto two, or reflect across a line. Each of these is a linear transformation.

Property I

Monomorphism (Injective)

No two inputs map to the same output. Information is never lost. The kernel contains only the zero vector. Different inputs remain distinguishable after transformation.

Property II

Epimorphism (Surjective)

Every possible output is reachable. The transformation covers the entire codomain. The image equals the whole target space. No gaps, no unreachable regions.

Property III — The Ideal

Isomorphism (Bijective)

Both injective and surjective simultaneously. Every input maps to a unique output and every output is reachable. The transformation is perfectly reversible. The two spaces are structurally identical — just seen from different coordinate systems.

Chapter 08

Equivalence, Similarity & SVD

The same transformation can be written in infinitely many ways. The art is finding the simplest one — the representation where the transformation's true nature is finally visible.

If A is a matrix and we find invertible matrices P and Q such that B = PAQ, then A and B are called equivalent. They represent the same transformation, viewed from different coordinate systems on both input and output sides. The simplest equivalent form — called the normal form — has a block of identity in the top-left corner and zeros everywhere else. Its rank is written on its face.

If instead we restrict to a single invertible matrix P such that D = P⁻¹AP, then A and D are called similar. This is a stricter relationship: same space on both sides, same basis change applied to input and output. The simplest similar form is diagonal. Its diagonal entries are the eigenvalues of A.

Concept Formula What's Preserved Normal Form
Equivalence B = PAQ Rank only Iᵣ in top-left, zeros elsewhere
Similarity D = P⁻¹AP Eigenvalues, trace, determinant, characteristic polynomial Diagonal matrix diag(λ₁, λ₂, …)
SVD A = UΣVᵀ Everything — complete decomposition Diagonal Σ, with orthogonal U and V

The Singular Value Decomposition is the culmination: every matrix A, regardless of shape, can be written as A = UΣVᵀ where U and V are orthogonal matrices (pure rotations) and Σ is a diagonal matrix of singular values. The anatomy of any transformation is, at root: rotate the input → scale each axis independently → rotate the output. Nothing more complex than that.

Importantly, the matrices P, Q, U, V are never chosen arbitrarily. They are forced by the structure of the problem. Equivalence forces P and Q from the kernel and image. Similarity forces P to be the eigenvectors — because diagonalisation algebraically requires Avᵢ = λᵢvᵢ. SVD forces V from the eigenvectors of AᵀA — because that matrix measures precisely how much A stretches each input direction.

Chapter 09

Orthogonality — The Cleanest Tool

Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero. Three letters. Profound consequences.

Perpendicular vectors share no common direction. They are maximally independent — not just linearly independent but geometrically independent. An orthogonal matrix is one whose columns form an orthonormal set: all pairs perpendicular, all lengths equal to one.

For an orthogonal matrix Q, the transpose equals the inverse: Qᵀ = Q⁻¹. This is the algebraic miracle of orthogonality — inversion, which normally costs O(n³) operations, becomes free: just flip the matrix. This happens because QᵀQ computes all pairwise dot products of columns, which are all zero (for off-diagonal entries) or one (on the diagonal) — giving the identity.

An orthogonal transformation does not stretch. It does not scale. It preserves every length and every angle. It is a pure rotation (or reflection). This means numerical errors cannot be amplified — a property of immense practical importance in computation.

Orthogonal transformations change your viewpoint without distorting your data. They are the safest moves in all of linear algebra.

Chapter 10

Affine Space — Freeing the Origin

Vector spaces have a sacred origin. Affine spaces ask: why should any point be special?

In a vector space, the zero vector is privileged. Every object is secretly a displacement from the origin. But in the real world, locations exist independently of any coordinate system. The temperature difference between today and yesterday is 5°C — a meaningful vector. But 20°C + 25°C does not equal 45°C in any useful sense. Points and displacements are fundamentally different objects.

An affine space formalises this distinction. It has a set of points with no algebraic structure, a vector space of displacements, and one connecting operation: point + vector = point. You can move a location by a displacement. You can find the displacement between two locations. But you cannot add two locations, and you cannot scale a location.

The beautiful fact: an affine space is a vector space that has forgotten where its origin is. Pick any point, declare it the origin, and you recover a vector space. Someone else picks a different origin — gets a different but equally valid vector space. The affine space is what both share: the structure that exists before anyone chooses coordinates.

// Allowed in affine space:
point + vector = point ✓
point − point = vector ✓
vector + vector = vector ✓

// Forbidden (undefined):
point + point = ??? ✗
scalar × point = ??? ✗

// Exception — affine combination (weights summing to 1):
0.5 × P + 0.5 × Q = midpoint ✓ // origin cancels out

Chapter 11

Where It All Lives: Machine Learning & Deep Learning

Every concept in this essay appears, concretely and critically, in the systems that are reshaping the world.

A neural network is a composition of linear transformations and nonlinearities. Every single layer computes y = Wx + b — a matrix multiplication (linear transformation) followed by a bias shift (the affine part) followed by an activation function (the nonlinearity). Without the nonlinearity, stacking a hundred layers collapses into a single matrix. The activation function is what makes depth meaningful.

The connection goes deeper. A matrix is a sum of rank-one pieces — each piece a column times a row, carrying exactly one direction of information. A neural network layer does the same thing with neurons: each neuron learns one direction of the composite function being approximated. The Universal Approximation Theorem is the neural network version of SVD — given enough pieces, any function can be reconstructed.

  • PCA — Principal Component Analysis Diagonalises the covariance matrix via similarity transformation. The eigenvectors are the principal axes. The eigenvalues tell you how much variance each direction contains. Used everywhere data must be compressed or visualised.
  • SVD — Image Compression & Recommendations Any matrix = sum of rank-1 pieces. Keep only the top-k pieces. Netflix's recommendation engine, image compression, and document similarity all run on this idea. The singular values rank the pieces by importance.
  • LoRA — Fine-tuning Large Language Models Instead of updating a full weight matrix W (billions of parameters), approximate the update as ΔW = BAᵀ using two thin low-rank matrices. A direct application of the observation that rank measures the true degrees of freedom. GPT, LLaMA, and Gemini are fine-tuned this way.
  • Attention Mechanism — Transformers The Q, K, V matrices in self-attention are linear projections of the input. The attention pattern is a similarity matrix (dot products measure alignment). The whole mechanism is linear algebra — weighted combinations of value vectors.
  • Orthogonal Weight Initialisation Initialising neural network weights as orthogonal matrices prevents vanishing and exploding gradients during training. Since orthogonal matrices preserve lengths, gradient magnitudes stay stable as they flow backwards through layers.
  • Graph Neural Networks — Spectral Methods The graph Laplacian matrix is analysed through its eigenvalues. Similar graph structures produce similar eigenvalue spectra. Matrix similarity in the formal linear algebra sense classifies graph topology in GNNs.
  • Normalising Flows — Generative Models An entire class of generative models built on the requirement that every transformation is an isomorphism — perfectly invertible bijections. Probability densities can be computed exactly because the transformations are reversible.
  • Computer Graphics — Affine Transformations Translating, rotating, and scaling 3D objects requires affine transformations. Translation cannot be expressed as a linear map. The solution — homogeneous coordinates — embeds affine operations into a higher-dimensional linear space, a beautiful trick.

You were not learning a calculation tool.
You were learning the geometry
of how information flows.

From a single equation with one unknown, to systems with a thousand variables, to the mathematical heart of language models — the thread is unbroken. Each concept was forced into existence by a problem the previous one couldn't solve. And each kept the same core idea: build complex things from simple building blocks, and find the coordinate system where the truth becomes simple.

The journey from 2x = 6 to the attention mechanism of a large language model is, in retrospect, a single continuous idea: find the right representation and the problem dissolves.

Vectors gave us a language for direction. Matrices gave us a machine for transformation. Rank gave us a measure of information. Eigenvalues gave us the natural axes of a transformation. SVD gave us the complete anatomy of any linear map. Orthogonality gave us the cleanest possible coordinate changes. Affine space freed us from the tyranny of the origin.

Every abstraction was paid for by a concrete problem that couldn't be solved without it. And every abstraction, once made, immediately revealed itself to be not just a solution but a window — a new way of seeing that made the next problem visible.

That is what mathematics always does. It does not just solve problems. It changes what you can see.
