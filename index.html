سفرنامہ  ·  सफ़रनामा

We don't read and write poetry because it's cute. We read and write poetry because we are members of the human race. And the human race is filled with passion. And medicine, law, business, engineering — these are noble pursuits and necessary to sustain life. But poetry, beauty, romance, love — these are what we stay alive for.

To quote from Whitman — "O me! O life!... of the questions of these recurring; of the endless trains of the faithless... of cities filled with the foolish; what good amid these, O me, O life?"

Answer. That you are here — that life exists, and identity; that the powerful play goes on and you may contribute a verse.

What will your verse be? — N.H. Kleinbaum, Dead Poets Society

Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening

Robert Frost

A traveler pauses mid-journey to watch snow fall silently into a dark wood. The scene holds him — its stillness almost seductive. But he cannot stay. He has promises to keep, miles to go, a life that demands he move on. It is not a poem about rest. It is a meditation on the tension between longing and duty — and its closing lines are not a complaint but a weary, resolute affirmation of the need to carry on.

The woods are lovely, dark and deep,
But I have promises to keep,
And miles to go before I sleep,
And miles to go before I sleep.

Find the ones worth suffering for

Bob Marley

Marley is laying out a kind of emotional logic here. Anything worth having will cost you something. Ease and worth rarely occupy the same space. The final turn is the most honest — he does not promise you a love without pain. He only asks you to find the people whose pain is worth carrying. That is not cynicism. That is wisdom from someone who understood that love is not the absence of suffering but the presence of someone worth suffering for.

If she's amazing, she won't be easy.
If she's easy, she won't be amazing.
If she's worth it, you won't give up.
If you give up, you're not worthy.

Truth is, everybody is going to hurt you;
you just gotta find the ones worth suffering for.

Manushyata — मनुष्यता

Maithilisharan Gupt

1910

Yeh kavita ek sawaal poochti hai — insaan aur janwar mein fark kya hai? Gupt ji kehte hain ki jo sirf apne liye jeeta hai, woh toh bas ek pashu hai. Asli insaan woh hai jo doosron ke liye jeeta hai, doosron ke liye marta hai. Kavita mein unhone Dadhichi, Karna, Raja Ushinar jaisi mahaan aatmaon ka zikr kiya hai jinhone apna sab kuch doosron ke liye de diya. Har stanza ek hi pankti par khatam hota hai — yeh repetition ek dhol ki tarah dil mein bajti hai. Yeh sirf ek kavita nahi, insaaniyat ki ek definition hai.

विचार लो कि मर्त्य हो न मृत्यु से डरो कभी,
मरो परंतु यों मरो कि याद जो करें सभी।
हुई न यों सुमृत्यु तो वृथा मरे, वृथा जिए,
मरा नहीं वही कि जो जिया न आपके लिए।
वही पशु-प्रवृत्ति है कि आप आप ही चरे,

उसी उदार की कथा सरस्वती बखानती,

उसी उदार की सदा सजीव कीर्ति कूजती;
तथा उसी उदार को समस्त सृष्टि पूजती।
अखंड आत्म भाव जो असीम विश्व में भरे,

क्षुधार्त रतिदेव ने दिया करस्थ थाल भी,
तथा दधीचि ने दिया परार्थ अस्थिजाल भी।
उशीनर क्षितीश ने स्वमांस दान भी किया,

अनित्य देह के लिए अनादि जीव क्या डरे?
वही मनुष्य है कि जो मनुष्य के लिए मरे॥

सहानुभूति चाहिए, महाविभूति है यही;
वशीकृता सदैव है बनी हुई स्वयं मही।
विरुद्धवाद बुद्ध का दया-प्रवाह में बहा,
विनीत लोकवर्ग क्या न सामने झुका रहा?
अहा! वही उदार है परोपकार जो करे,
वही मनुष्य है कि जो मनुष्य के लिए मरे॥

रहो न भूले के कभी मदांध तुच्छ वित्त में,
सनाथ जान आपको करो न गर्व चित्त में।
अनाथ कौन है यहाँ? त्रिलोकनाथ साथ हैं,
दयालु दीनबंधु के बड़े विशाल हाथ हैं।
अतीव भाग्यहीन है अधीर भाव जो करे,
वही मनुष्य है कि जो मनुष्य के लिए मरे॥

अनंत अंतरिक्ष में अनंत देव हैं खड़े,
समक्ष ही स्वबाहु जो बढ़ा रहे बड़े-बड़े।
परस्परावलंब से उठो तथा बढ़ो सभी,
रहो न यों कि एक से न काम और का सरे,
वही मनुष्य है कि जो मनुष्य के लिए मरे॥

'मनुष्य मात्र बंधु है' यही बड़ा विवेक है,

फलानुसार कर्म के अवश्य बाह्य भेद है,

तभी समर्थ भाव है कि तारता हुआ तरे,
वही मनुष्य है कि जो मनुष्य के लिए मरे॥

منزلیں لاکھ کٹھن

ساقی امروہوی

Manzilein Lakh Kathin is a ghazal of defiance and loneliness walking hand in hand. Saqi Amrohvi declares that no matter how difficult the road gets, he will keep moving — because sitting down in defeat is its own kind of death. But beneath that resolve lives a quiet pain. The people who once walked beside him, who predicted he would fall apart midway, are nowhere to be seen now. Home never felt like home. The world has been unkind. Yet the line that stays with you is this — wherever the light goes, I will follow. The final sher carries the softest wound of the whole ghazal — love never suited him either. He had imagined he would find his way into every heart. It did not happen. And he says it without bitterness, just a gentle acknowledgment of what was lost. This is not a poem of triumph. It is something more honest — the portrait of a man who keeps going not because the road is easy, but because stopping is not something he knows how to do.

English Quotes

Khalil Gibran
Out of suffering have emerged the strongest souls; the most massive characters are seared with scars.

Gibran is not consoling you here — he is making an observation about the architecture of great human beings. Suffering is not something that happens to strong souls despite their strength. It is precisely what forged that strength in the first place. The scars are not evidence of damage. They are evidence of survival, and of the fire that was survived. A soul untouched by pain remains unformed, like metal that was never put through heat.

Khalil Gibran
I deserted the world and sought solitude because I became tired of rendering courtesy to those multitudes who believe that humility is a sort of weakness, and mercy a kind of cowardice, and snobbery is a form of strength.

This is Gibran's quiet resignation letter to society. He did not leave the world out of bitterness or defeat — he left because he refused to keep pretending that cruelty was strength and gentleness was weakness. The world he describes is one that has its values completely inverted. Solitude, for him, was not loneliness. It was the only honest place left to stand.

Khalil Gibran
Travel and tell no one. Live a true love story and tell no one. Live happily and tell no one. People ruin beautiful things.

There is something deeply protective in this quote. Gibran understood that the moment you share something sacred with the world, the world begins to have opinions about it. Other people's doubts, judgments, and envy have a way of entering the cracks of something beautiful and slowly widening them. Some things stay alive only in silence. The most precious chapters of a life are often the ones nobody else ever read.

Franz Kafka
A first sign of the beginning of understanding is the wish to die.

This is perhaps the most unsettling thing Kafka ever wrote — and that is saying something. He is not glorifying death. He is describing a specific moment of awakening when a person first truly sees the world as it is — its absurdity, its indifference, its weight — and feels crushed by that clarity. The wish to die is, paradoxically, a sign of intelligence. It means the comfortable illusions have finally broken. What comes after that breaking, if one survives it, is real understanding.

Sergei Yesenin
In life, you can search for everything except love and death. They find you when the time comes.

Yesenin spent his short life chasing both — love with a desperate intensity, and death which arrived at thirty years old. This quote carries the weight of someone who learned this truth firsthand. Love and death operate outside the reach of human will. You cannot schedule them, manufacture them, or avoid them. Every other thing in life rewards effort and intention. These two arrive on their own terms, at their own hour, and there is a strange peace in accepting that.

Buddhism

The Middle Way  ·  मध्यम मार्ग

Siddhartha Gautama  ·  5th century BCE  ·  Ancient India

The Central Insight

Buddhism begins with one unflinching observation — life as ordinarily lived is dukkha (दुःख). Not occasionally. Structurally. The Buddha did not say this to be pessimistic. He said it the way a doctor states a diagnosis — clearly, so that treatment can begin.

Dukkha is usually translated as suffering but the more honest translation is unsatisfactoriness — a wheel whose axle is slightly off center. Everything feels subtly wrong because we are living in a fundamental misunderstanding of what we are and how reality actually works.

The Buddha was not a god, not a prophet, not a messenger of divine revelation. He was a human being who sat down under a tree and looked directly at his own mind until he understood it completely. What he found, he spent the rest of his life teaching — not as commandments, but as an invitation to look for yourself.

The Four Noble Truths  ·  चार आर्य सत्य

These are not beliefs to be accepted. They are observations to be verified in one's own experience. The Buddha structured them deliberately like a medical diagnosis — identify the illness, find its cause, confirm that cure is possible, prescribe the path.

First Truth  ·  Dukkha (दुःख)

Suffering exists. Life contains pain, loss, impermanence, and a pervasive unsatisfactoriness. This is not a belief or a religious claim. It is a direct observation — the kind anyone can verify simply by looking honestly at their own experience. The pleasant things end. The painful things arrive uninvited. Even in moments of happiness there is an undercurrent of anxiety about losing it.

Second Truth  ·  Samudaya (समुदाय)

Suffering has an origin. That origin is tanha — craving, literally thirst. We crave pleasure, crave existence itself, crave for things to be different from what they are. This craving is not a character flaw. It is the default operating mode of an untrained mind. It reaches for the pleasant, recoils from the unpleasant, and generates endless suffering in the gap between what is and what it wants.

Third Truth  ·  Nirodha (निरोध)

Suffering can end. The complete cessation of craving is possible. This is the most radical claim Buddhism makes — that the human mind can be fundamentally and permanently freed from its own self-generated torment. This state of liberation is called Nirvana (निर्वाण) — literally the extinguishing of a flame. Not annihilation. Not bliss in a cloud. The simple, clear absence of the fire that was burning all along.

Fourth Truth  ·  Magga (मार्ग)

There is a path to that cessation. It is not a miracle, not a grace, not something given from outside. It is a practice — the Ashtangika Marga, the Eightfold Path. Suffering ends not through belief but through a systematic transformation of how one sees, thinks, speaks, acts, and pays attention.

The Three Marks of Existence  ·  तीन लक्षण

These are the three characteristics that Buddhism says apply to all conditioned reality — everything that arises, exists within time, and depends on causes and conditions.

Anicca (अनित्य)  ·  Impermanence

Everything that arises passes away. Every thought, every feeling, every person, every civilization, every universe. Nothing holds its shape. The suffering we manufacture for ourselves comes largely from trying to hold on to what is already in the process of leaving — clinging to the pleasant, resisting the unpleasant, as though either clinging or resistance could change the fundamental nature of things.

Dukkha (दुःख)  ·  Unsatisfactoriness

Because everything is impermanent, nothing can provide lasting satisfaction. We keep reaching for things — relationships, achievements, experiences, possessions — expecting them to finally be enough. They never are. Not because the things are bad, but because permanence is what the craving mind demands and impermanence is what reality delivers. The gap between those two is dukkha.

Anatta (अनात्मा)  ·  No Fixed Self

This is the most radical and difficult idea in all of Buddhism. There is no permanent, unchanging self behind your experiences. What you call "I" is actually a constantly changing stream of sensations, thoughts, emotions, and perceptions — with no fixed experiencer standing behind them. The self is a process, not a thing. A river, not a rock. Most of the suffering we carry comes from defending, decorating, and mourning a self that does not ultimately exist in the way we imagine it does.

The Eightfold Path  ·  अष्टांगिक मार्ग

Not commandments. Not rules handed down from a god. A map drawn by someone who walked the territory himself. The eight factors are not sequential steps — they are eight dimensions of a single integrated way of living, grouped into three trainings.

Prajna  ·  प्रज्ञा  ·  Wisdom
Right Understanding Samma Ditthi

Seeing reality as it actually is — impermanent, interdependent, without a fixed self at the center. Not as a philosophical position but as a living, moment-to-moment recognition.

Right Intention Samma Sankappa

The commitment to renunciation, to non-ill-will, to non-cruelty. The direction of the heart before any action is taken.

Sila  ·  शील  ·  Ethics
Right Speech Samma Vaca

Speaking truthfully, kindly, usefully, and at the right time. Recognizing that words are not neutral — they shape the minds that speak them as much as the minds that hear them.

Right Action Samma Kammanta

Acting in ways that do not harm oneself or others. Refraining from taking life, from taking what is not given, from misconduct that damages trust and causes suffering.

Right Livelihood Samma Ajiva

Earning one's living in a way that does not cause harm. How we spend our working hours is how we spend a large portion of our lives — it cannot be separated from the path.

Samadhi  ·  समाधि  ·  Meditation
Right Effort Samma Vayama

The sustained energy to cultivate wholesome states of mind and abandon unwholesome ones. Not force or strain — the steady, patient effort of a gardener, not a soldier.

Right Mindfulness Samma Sati

Clear, continuous awareness of what is actually happening in this moment — in the body, in feelings, in the mind, in the nature of experience itself. This is the heart of Buddhist practice.

Right Concentration Samma Samadhi

The cultivation of a unified, stable, deeply collected mind through meditation. The still water in which reality can finally be seen clearly, without the constant distortion of agitation and craving.

Introduction

Everything here is borrowed — stolen from authors I've stayed up reading, films that quietly wrecked me, and songs that arrived at exactly the right moment. Safarnama is not my writing. It is my listening. A record of everything that passed through me and left a mark.

What's in a name? That which we call a rose by any other name would smell as sweet. — William Shakespeare

So if you ask who I am — I am simply what moved me.
